Lighthousenavigation

A lighthouse is a tower or other structure that emits light from a system of lamps and lenses to serve as a navigational aid for maritime pilots at sea or on inland waterways. Lighthouses mark dangerous coastlines, hazardous shoals and reefs, and safe entries to harbours; they also assist in aerial navigation. Once ubiquitous, their number has fallen with the rise of electronic navigation systems.

Historyheritage

The most famous lighthouse of antiquity was the Pharos of Alexandria, built in the 3rd century BC and counted among the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. It stood for more than a millennium before earthquakes brought it down. The word for lighthouse in several Romance languages still derives from its name.

The Pharos gave its light to sailors far out at sea, and its flame, it was said, could be seen from a day's voyage away.

The age of Fresnel

The decisive leap came in 1822, when the French physicist Augustin-Jean Fresnel introduced the lens that bears his name. By replacing a solid lens with a set of concentric prismatic rings, the Fresnel lens captured far more of a lamp's light and threw it in a tight, far-reaching beam. A single oil flame could suddenly be seen tens of kilometres away.

Constructionengineering

Where a lighthouse sits decides how it is built. On firm headland a simple masonry tower suffices; on a reef awash at high tide, engineers face one of the hardest problems in civil engineering.

Wave-swept towers

The classic answer is the interlocking stone tower, pioneered at the Eddystone and perfected at the Bell Rock. Each granite block is dovetailed to its neighbours so the whole tower behaves as a single monolith against the sea.

Common building materials, roughly in order of adoption:

  • Wood and rubble, for early and temporary lights
  • Dressed stone and granite, for wave-swept towers
    • Dovetailed courses for interlock
    • A solid base storey for ballast
  • Cast iron, bolted in prefabricated plates
  • Reinforced concrete, in the modern era

A wave-swept tower is raised in stages:

  1. Survey the rock and cut a level foundation socket
  2. Land and dress each numbered block on shore
  3. Set the courses, dovetailing every block to the last
  4. Fit the lantern room and install the optic

Optics and characteristic

Every lighthouse flashes a distinct pattern, its characteristic, so a navigator can tell one light from the next. The pattern is written in a compact shorthand on charts; for example a light that flashes white twice every ten seconds is noted as: 

Fl(2) W 10s 40m 18M

read as Fl(2) two flashes, W white, every 10s, with the lamp 40m above the sea and a nominal range of 18M (nautical miles). The elements of a characteristic are:

  • Rhythm — fixed, flashing, occulting, or isophase
  • Colour — white, red, or green
  • Period — the seconds for one full cycle

Note

Range depends on both the light's intensity and the height of the observer's eye, so the charted range is only nominal.

Tip

Red sectors are often used to warn of a specific hazard: see red, and you are standing into danger.

Warning

Never navigate on a single light alone; cross-check the characteristic against the chart and a second mark.

Automation and decline

Through the late twentieth century, keepers were replaced by automatic lamps, sun switches, and remote monitoring. The last manned light in many countries was automated in the 1990s, ending a profession that had passed through families for generations.

The light keeps its watch whether anyone keeps the light.
